# Governing Agentic AI: The Research Landscape in 2026

**The academic and industry research community has converged on a clear message: agentic AI governance requires fundamentally new frameworks, not extensions of existing AI governance.** Between 2024 and 2026, research output on agent safety, trust, architecture, observability, and risk exploded — producing actionable frameworks from NIST, OWASP, CSA, DeepMind, and Anthropic, alongside formal academic contributions on delegation, corrigibility, runtime verification, and multi-agent failure modes. The critical finding for practitioners is that **no single standard will be finalized before enterprise agent adoption reaches 40% penetration** (Gartner, projected end of 2026), meaning governance frameworks must be built now from complementary, already-available components. This report maps the research landscape across five domains, assesses maturity levels, identifies consensus and debate, and extracts practical guidance for building an Architecture Framework for Agentic Systems (AFAS).

---

## 1. Agent safety research has produced its first empirical foundations

The field moved decisively from theoretical safety arguments to empirical measurement in 2025–2026, anchored by three landmark contributions.

**DeepMind's "Intelligent AI Delegation" framework** (Tomašev, Franklin & Osindero, arXiv:2602.11865, February 2026) formalizes delegation as a nine-component system: task decomposition, task assignment, multi-objective optimization, adaptive coordination, monitoring, trust and reputation, permission handling, verifiable task completion (using cryptographic proofs), and security. The paper introduces an **11-axis task taxonomy** — complexity, criticality, uncertainty, duration, cost, resource requirements, constraints, verifiability, reversibility, contextuality, and subjectivity — each axis influencing appropriate delegation protocols. For AFAS, the framework's direct mapping to zero trust is notable: permission handling requires capability-based access control; trust systems use cryptographic attestations; monitoring adapts between periodic and event-triggered modes based on task criticality. The paper remains theoretical but provides the most comprehensive formal vocabulary for delegation governance to date.

**Anthropic's "Measuring AI Agent Autonomy in Practice"** (February 2026) is the strongest empirical contribution to date, analyzing millions of real human-agent interactions across Claude Code and Anthropic's API. Key quantitative findings reshape governance assumptions: the **99.9th percentile autonomous turn duration nearly doubled** from under 25 minutes to over 45 minutes between October 2025 and January 2026; **73% of API tool calls have human-in-the-loop oversight**; only **0.8% of actions are irreversible**; and experienced users simultaneously auto-approve more sessions (20% → 40%) while also interrupting more often (5% → 9% of turns), demonstrating sophisticated trust calibration rather than complacency. The study confirmed a significant "deployment overhang" — models are capable of far more autonomy than users grant. This validates graduated trust models and argues for conservative default autonomy settings.

**Anthropic's alignment auditing agents** (July 2025) demonstrated that automated auditing can scale human oversight: an evaluation agent discriminated aligned from misaligned models in **88% of runs**, while an investigator agent identified misalignment root causes 10–13% of the time individually and **42% with super-agent aggregation**. Separately, the Anthropic-OpenAI joint alignment evaluation (2025) found all tested models from both developers struggled with sycophancy, and some exhibited concerning behavior around misuse scenarios — providing cross-organizational empirical evidence that behavioral evaluation alone is insufficient.

At Berkeley, CHAI contributed formal game-theoretic work on multi-agent corrigibility (Dable-Heath et al., arXiv:2501.05360, January 2025), proving that **uncertainty over human preferences can keep agents corrigible** even when humans act irrationally — a formal basis for "never trust by default." The CAST framework (arXiv:2506.03056, June 2025) proposed corrigibility as a single training objective, creating an "attractor basin" where agents become instrumentally motivated to empower principals. Stanford HAI's 2025 Foundation Model Transparency Index scored 13 developers across **100 transparency indicators**, driving measurable disclosure improvements (16.6 new indicators per company on average after each edition), while the 2025 AI Index Report documented a **56.4% surge in AI incidents** to a record 233 in 2024.

For multi-agent safety specifically, the **MAST framework** (Cemri et al., arXiv:2503.13657, NeurIPS 2025) provided the first comprehensive empirical taxonomy of multi-agent system failures, analyzing 1,642 execution traces across seven frameworks. Failure rates ranged from **41% to 86.7%**, with 14 distinct failure modes identified. Inter-agent misalignment accounted for **36.9% of all failures** — including conversation resets, wrong assumptions, and role disobedience. A complementary DeepMind study (Yubin Kim et al., December 2025) showed unstructured multi-agent networks amplify errors up to **17.2x** compared to single-agent baselines. The ODCV-Bench (arXiv:2512.20798, December 2025) introduced 40 scenarios distinguishing incentivized misalignment (agent independently derives unethical strategy) from mandated misalignment (following harmful instructions), finding non-zero misalignment rates in both categories across frontier models. The Gradient Institute's report for the Australian government identified six key failure modes in governed multi-agent environments, warning that purely inter-agent communication may become unintelligible to human supervisors over time.

## 2. Trust calibration research reveals human oversight is far more fragile than assumed

The trust and delegation literature converges on a finding that should alarm any governance practitioner: **human oversight alone is demonstrably insufficient**, and the research quantifies exactly how insufficient it is.

Feng, McDonald & Zhang (University of Washington, arXiv:2506.12469, July 2025) defined **five levels of agent autonomy** — L1 Operator through L5 Observer — adopted by the 2025 MIT AI Agent Index, which found chat agents operating at L1–L3, enterprise agents at L2–L4, and browser agents at L4–L5. The framework proposes "autonomy certificates" issued by third-party governing bodies prescribing maximum autonomy levels — a concept directly analogous to safety certifications in aviation or medical devices, and immediately relevant to AFAS as a compliance mechanism.

Research on scalable oversight produced sobering results. Engels et al. (arXiv:2504.18530, April 2025) modeled oversight as a game between capability-mismatched players and tested four protocols (Mafia, Debate, Backdoor Code, Wargames). At a 400-Elo capability gap, the **best protocol (Debate) achieved only 51.7% success**, with rates declining for stronger systems. Nested Scalable Oversight success rates for other protocols were worse: Mafia 13.5%, Backdoor Code 10.0%, Wargames 9.4%. A complementary benchmark (Sudhir et al., arXiv:2504.03731, 2025) introduced the Agent Score Difference metric showing that while debate significantly outperforms baselines at incentivizing truth-telling, the absolute success rates remain limited.

Multiple 2025 studies documented that humans in oversight roles provide correct oversight approximately **50% of the time**, with automation bias causing clinicians to override their own correct decisions in favor of erroneous AI advice in **6% of cases**. The Responsible AI Foundation concluded that "where you place humans in the workflow matters far more than whether you place them there at all." Google DeepMind's comprehensive 145-page technical safety roadmap (Shah et al., arXiv:2504.01849, April 2025) explicitly acknowledged that human oversight alone is insufficient for sufficiently capable systems, proposing "amplified oversight" that leverages AI systems themselves to help oversee stronger AI, combined with system-level security through monitoring and access control.

The **Trust Calibration Maturity Model** (Steinmetz et al., arXiv:2503.15511, 2025) offers practitioners a five-dimensional assessment: Performance Characterization, Bias & Robustness Quantification, Transparency, Safety & Security, and Usability — each with escalating maturity levels. Empirical work on trust calibration (McGrath et al., Frontiers in AI, May 2025; Stocker et al., ICIS 2025) validated that trust varies at the feature level rather than being a uniform system-level construct, and that short-form measurement instruments enable repeated trust assessment during interactions, enabling dynamic trust calibration.

For adaptive autonomy, the **HAIF framework** (arXiv:2602.07641, 2026) documented a "productivity paradox" where senior developers experienced approximately **19% performance decreases** on complex AI-assisted tasks due to verification overhead, and identified a "two-speed IT" problem where AI-accelerated generation outpaces organizational validation capacity. The ATLAS project (UC Berkeley, Spring 2026) demonstrated translating EU AI Act Article 14 and NIST AI RMF requirements into enforceable technical controls, providing a prototype governance layer for high-risk AI systems.

The practical implication for AFAS is clear: governance frameworks must implement **defense-in-depth oversight** combining human checkpoints, AI-assisted monitoring, automated auditing, and runtime constraints — never relying on any single oversight mechanism.

## 3. Agent identity and zero trust have reached an inflection point

The agent architecture and identity space underwent a transformation in 2025–2026, shifting from theoretical proposals to deployable standards and commercial products, with zero trust emerging as the dominant governance paradigm.

**NIST launched its AI Agent Standards Initiative on February 17, 2026**, accompanied by an RFI on AI Agent Security and a concept paper on agent identity and authorization from the NCCoE. The concept paper ("Accelerating Adoption of Software and AI Agent Identity and Authorization," Galluzzo et al., February 2026) established that **AI agents should be identifiable entities in enterprise identity systems**, with focus areas spanning identification, authentication, authorization, auditing, and prompt injection prevention. It references OAuth 2.0/2.1, OpenID Connect, SPIFFE/SPIRE, SCIM, and NGAC as candidate standards, mapped against NIST SP 800-207 (Zero Trust) and SP 800-63-4 (Digital Identity).

The **OpenID Foundation whitepaper** ("Identity Management for Agentic AI," Tobin South et al., October 2025) diagnosed that OAuth 2.1 works within single trust domains but breaks down for cross-domain, asynchronous, and highly autonomous agent interactions. Critical challenges include identity fragmentation, user impersonation through delegated authority, consent fatigue, recursive delegation, and browser agents breaking authorization models. The paper recommends SSO/SCIM for agent lifecycle management, IPSIE profiles, explicit "on-behalf-of" flows, and proposes an Agent Naming Service for secure discovery.

The **CSA Agentic Trust Framework** (Woodruff et al., February 2026, with a foreword by Zero Trust creator John Kindervag) represents the most directly applicable zero trust governance framework for AI agents. It introduces a **human-role-metaphor maturity model** — Intern (read-only) → Associate → Senior → Staff → Principal — with explicit promotion criteria requiring time-at-level, performance thresholds, security validation, and governance sign-off. The framework is open-source and Fortune 500-tested. It complements the **CSA MAESTRO framework** (Ken Huang, February 2025), which provides seven-layer threat modeling extending STRIDE, PASTA, and LINDDUN with AI-specific threat categories.

**SAGA** (Syros et al., arXiv:2504.21034, April 2025, accepted at ACM CCS 2025/NDSS 2026) is the **first fully implemented and evaluated security architecture** for governing agentic systems, providing provider-based user and agent registration, cryptographic access control tokens with formal security guarantees, and fine-grained per-interaction token management. It scales to millions of agents with minimal overhead and integrates with the A2A protocol. On the commercial side, **CyberArk** launched the first purpose-built identity security solution for AI agents (December 2025), **Microsoft Entra Agent ID** extended zero trust to the "agentic workforce" (May 2025, expanded January 2026), and **WSO2** introduced agent-first identity models. Yet a Strata/CSA survey of 285 IT/security professionals found only **23% have formal agent identity strategies** and **53% still use static API keys**.

The **OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications** (December 2025, 100+ contributors) established the definitive risk taxonomy: Goal Hijack, Tool Misuse, Identity & Privilege Abuse, Supply Chain Vulnerabilities, Impact Chain/Blast Radius, Knowledge Base Poisoning, Inter-Agent Communication, Cascading Failures, Data Leakage, and Rogue Agents. Three of the top four risks revolve around identities, tools, and delegated trust boundaries — validating identity-centric governance.

Protocol security remains a critical gap. Analysis of **5,200+ MCP servers** (Astrix, 2025) found 88% require credentials but **53% use insecure static keys** and only 8.5% use OAuth. Palo Alto Unit 42 documented three MCP attack vectors: resource theft, conversation hijacking, and covert tool invocation. Real-world MCP supply chain attacks emerged in September 2025, including a malicious Postmark-impersonating server that BCC'd all emails, and 126 compromised packages with 86,000 downloads. The A2A protocol (Google, April 2025; Linux Foundation, June 2025) addresses agent-to-agent communication with JWT/OIDC authentication but still has gaps in enforced short-lived tokens, consent mechanisms, and Agent Card spoofing.

## 4. Observability infrastructure is the most critical gap between research and practice

Research on monitoring, provenance, and runtime verification produced some of the most immediately deployable results, yet actual enterprise adoption lags dramatically.

**AgentSpec** (Wang, Poskitt & Sun, ICSE 2026, arXiv:2503.18666) introduced a lightweight domain-specific language for specifying runtime constraints on LLM agents, with rules consisting of triggers, predicates, and enforcement mechanisms. It prevented **over 90% of unsafe code executions**, eliminated all hazardous embodied agent actions, and achieved 100% autonomous vehicle compliance with millisecond-level overhead. This is the most practical tool identified for translating governance policies into enforceable runtime constraints — essentially **policy-as-code for AI agents**. The complementary "Policy-as-Prompt" work (NeurIPS 2025 Workshop, arXiv:2509.23994) demonstrated an end-to-end pipeline converting technical artifacts into prompt-based classifiers enforcing least-privilege policies at runtime.

**AgentGuard** (Koohestani, ASE 2025 Workshop, arXiv:2509.23864) introduced Dynamic Probabilistic Assurance for runtime verification, using online learning to build and update Markov Decision Process models of emergent agent behavior, with probabilistic model checking providing real-time quantitative safety guarantees. This shifts verification from binary pass/fail to continuous probability assessment — a paradigm better suited to the inherent nondeterminism of LLM agents. **Meta's LlamaFirewall** (2025) provides an open-source, production-ready guardrail system with three components: PromptGuard 2 for jailbreak detection, Agent Alignment Checks for chain-of-thought auditing, and CodeShield for online static analysis.

For anomaly detection, **SentinelAgent** (He et al., arXiv:2505.24201, May 2025) models agent interactions as dynamic execution graphs with anomaly detection at node, edge, and path levels, achieving **92% accuracy** in identifying harmful agent behaviors including prompt injection propagation, unauthorized tool use, and multi-agent collusion. It uses OpenTelemetry for runtime event interception. A complementary Sentinel Agents architecture (Gosmar et al., arXiv:2509.14956, September 2025) provides a dual-layered system with distributed security agents and a coordinator agent managing policy implementation, alert ingestion, and quarantine of misbehaving agents.

**AgentSight** (arXiv:2508.02736, 2025) introduced eBPF-based boundary tracing that bridges the "semantic gap" between agent intent and system-level actions, intercepting TLS-encrypted LLM traffic to extract semantic intent while monitoring kernel events. Its Correlation Engine reduced 3,153 raw events to 37 merged events in one session, demonstrating that agent observability can be achieved without modifying agent code. **OpenTelemetry's GenAI Special Interest Group** is defining semantic conventions for AI agent telemetry, providing the vendor-neutral standard that governance frameworks need.

Provenance research advanced through **PROV-AGENT** (UT-Battelle/ORNL, arXiv:2508.02866, 2025), which extends the W3C PROV standard with Model Context Protocol concepts to represent agent actions and their connections to data and workflow tasks, enabling near-real-time provenance capture across edge, cloud, and HPC environments. The Data Foundation's 2025 report established data provenance as "infrastructure for innovation," noting federal agencies deploy over 1,700 AI use cases with a growing provenance gap.

Chain-of-thought monitoring research (Korbak et al., arXiv:2507.11473, 2025) found that **CoT monitoring is more effective than action-only monitoring** but is "fragile" — effectiveness may degrade under different training procedures, data sources, or scaling. The SHADE-Arena benchmark (Kutasov et al., arXiv:2506.15740, 2025) tested whether agents can sabotage tasks while evading monitoring, demonstrating that governance frameworks must account for adversarial evasion. And a new paradigm for agentic explainability (Zhu et al., arXiv:2601.17168, January 2026) argued that traditional XAI techniques are insufficient for agentic systems because they fail to capture temporal dynamics, compounding decisions, and emergent multi-agent behavior — requiring **trajectory-level explainability** rather than feature-attribution methods.

The **CoSAI Principles for Secure-by-Design Agentic Systems** (2025) synthesize industry consensus: all components and actions must be observable, behavior and provenance verifiable, with comprehensive telemetry enabling anomalous behavior detection at machine speed and immutable logs of inputs, reasoning, tool usage, communications, and outputs.

## 5. Risk assessment frameworks are converging but quantitative methods remain immature

The risk assessment landscape for agentic AI has consolidated around a complementary triad — **OWASP Agentic Top 10 for risk taxonomy, MAESTRO for threat modeling, and FAIR-AIR for quantification** — supplemented by government-backed frameworks from Singapore, the UK, and the EU.

The **MAESTRO framework** (Ken Huang, CSA, February 2025) provides seven-layer threat modeling: Foundation Models → Data Operations → Agent Frameworks → Deployment Infrastructure → Security & Compliance → Evaluation & Observability → Agent Ecosystem. It has been validated through academic implementation on network monitoring prototypes (Zambare et al., arXiv:2508.10043, August 2025), confirming novel threats including traffic replay DoS and memory poisoning via log tampering. **ASTRIDE** (Bandara et al., arXiv:2512.04785, December 2025) extends STRIDE with an "A" category for agent-specific attacks and automates threat modeling from visual architecture diagrams using fine-tuned vision-language models.

The **FAIR-AIR Playbook** (FAIR Institute, 2024–2025) represents the most mature adaptation of quantitative cyber risk assessment to AI, defining five vectors of GenAI risk (Shadow GenAI, Creating Own LLM, Hosting on LLMs, Managed LLMs, Active Cyber Attack) and producing **quantifiable outputs in dollar terms**. Dropbox deployed FAIR-based GenAI risk management in production, demonstrating enterprise viability. A hybrid FAIR-XGBoost framework (Expert Systems with Applications, 2025) showed Expected Annual Loss grows exponentially beyond Risk Exposure Score 0.4, with SHAP analysis identifying Primary Loss and Technology Score as key predictors. IBM's Quantitative AI Risk Assessments work (arXiv:2209.06317v3, updated December 2024) addresses the tension between standardization and customization in risk metrics but notes individual-versus-summary tradeoffs remain unresolved.

**Singapore's Agentic Risk and Capability (ARC) Framework** (GovTech, December 2025) is the most operationally complete government-backed framework, cataloging **46 risks and 88 controls** with risk-to-control mappings in an interactive risk register, benchmarked against NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act, OWASP, Google SAIF 2.0, and MAESTRO. Its ARCvisor tool provides AI-powered risk assessment achieving 50%+ time savings. Microsoft's Taxonomy of Failure Modes (April 2025) categorizes failures across safety and security pillars, identifying memory poisoning as "particularly insidious," while IBM's AI Risk Atlas provides machine-readable, open-source risk-to-benchmark-to-mitigation mappings.

Safety evaluation infrastructure matured significantly. **METR** (Model Evaluation and Threat Research) documented that AI agent task-length capability doubles approximately every **7 months**, with GPT-5's 50% time horizon at approximately 2 hours 17 minutes. METR's prompted monitors achieved AUROCs of **0.96 for reward hacking** and 0.91 for sandbagging detection. The **UK AI Security Institute** tested 30+ frontier models, released the open-source Inspect AI framework with 100+ pre-built evaluations, and launched ControlArena for AI control experiments. The International AI Safety Report (Bengio et al., 2025) documented leading AI performance improving from 18-minute to 2+ hour time horizons in a single year.

On the regulatory front, the **EU AI Act** entered phased enforcement in 2025 with full high-risk obligations applicable by August 2026 (fines up to EUR 35M or 7% of global turnover). Agent autonomy and tool use factor into systemic risk designation under Article 51, while Article 50 triggers transparency requirements for agents interacting with humans. **ISO/IEC 42001:2023** is the first certifiable AI management system standard, with 76% of organizations planning to pursue certification according to CSA's 2025 benchmark. However, it does not yet include agent-specific requirements. NIST's AI RMF ecosystem — AI RMF 1.0, AI 600-1 GenAI Profile, and the draft Cyber AI Profile (IR 8596, December 2025) — provides the US voluntary governance foundation, but agent-specific standards from NIST are not expected before 2027.

## 6. Where research consensus forms and where debate persists

The academic community has reached meaningful consensus on several governance-critical questions while significant debates remain unresolved.

**Areas of strong consensus include:** Agentic AI is categorically different from static model deployment and requires purpose-built governance (supported by OWASP, NIST, CSA, DeepMind, Anthropic, and virtually all academic work surveyed). Identity is foundational — agents must be first-class principals in identity systems (NIST NCCoE, OpenID Foundation, CSA ATF, Microsoft, CyberArk converge). Human oversight alone is insufficient; defense-in-depth combining human, AI-assisted, and automated oversight is necessary (DeepMind AGI safety roadmap, Anthropic, scalable oversight research, multiple empirical studies). Zero trust principles apply directly to agent governance, with "never trust, always verify" as the default posture. Observability is a prerequisite for governance — "you cannot govern what you cannot see" (McKinsey, CoSAI, OWASP ASI09).

**Active debates center on:** How much autonomy is safe to grant, and how to measure it — Anthropic's deployment overhang evidence suggests models are more capable than deployed, but the optimal autonomous operating point is unknown. Whether chain-of-thought monitoring can remain reliable as models become more capable — Korbak et al. show it works but is "fragile," and SHADE-Arena demonstrates adversarial evasion. The right abstraction for agent identity — DIDs/VCs (academic proposals), OAuth extensions (OpenID Foundation), or purpose-built solutions (commercial vendors). Whether governance should be centralized (orchestrator-enforced) or distributed (sentinel-agent patterns) — both approaches have empirical support. The feasibility of formal verification for inherently nondeterministic systems — AgentGuard's probabilistic approach represents a pragmatic middle ground.

**The research-practice gap is widest in four areas.** First, **quantitative risk assessment**: while FAIR-AIR exists, no peer-reviewed methodology connects agent-specific risk factors (autonomy level, tool access scope, irreversibility of actions) to probabilistic loss estimates — practitioners must improvise. Second, **agent identity at scale**: despite conceptual frameworks from NIST and OpenID Foundation, only 23% of organizations have formal agent identity strategies and 53% still use static API keys. Third, **multi-agent governance**: research documents failure rates of 41–87% in multi-agent systems, but no deployed governance framework addresses inter-agent coordination failures beyond basic orchestrator patterns. Fourth, **provenance and audit trails**: PROV-AGENT extends W3C PROV for agents, but no production-ready, agent-aware provenance system exists at enterprise scale, and Anthropic notes there is no reliable way to associate independent API requests into sessions of agentic activity.

## What a practical governance framework should incorporate

Based on this research synthesis, an Architecture Framework for Agentic Systems should integrate the following evidence-based elements.

**For trust and delegation**, adopt Feng et al.'s five-level autonomy model (L1–L5) as the classification backbone, map each level to DeepMind's 11-axis task taxonomy for delegation decisions, and implement the CSA ATF's role-metaphor maturity progression (Intern → Principal) with explicit promotion criteria. Never allow single-mechanism oversight — layer human checkpoints with AI-assisted monitoring (Anthropic's auditing agents), runtime constraints (AgentSpec), and anomaly detection (SentinelAgent).

**For identity and zero trust**, treat agents as first-class identity principals per NIST NCCoE guidance, implement scoped, short-lived credentials following OAuth 2.1/SPIFFE patterns, adopt the "least agency" principle from OWASP (never grant more autonomy than the business problem requires), and build toward the "Know Your Agent" paradigm with cryptographic identity, human binding, behavior measurement, and risk scoring.

**For observability**, mandate OpenTelemetry-based telemetry collection for all agent actions, implement trajectory-level explainability (not just feature attribution), deploy runtime verification using probabilistic approaches (AgentGuard) and policy-as-code (AgentSpec), extend W3C PROV for agent provenance, and build immutable audit trails covering inputs, reasoning, tool usage, communications, and outputs per CoSAI principles.

**For risk assessment**, use the OWASP Agentic Top 10 as the primary risk taxonomy, conduct threat modeling using MAESTRO's seven-layer architecture, supplement with Singapore's ARC Framework for risk-to-control mapping (46 risks, 88 controls), and adapt FAIR-AIR for financial quantification. Monitor METR's time-horizon metric for capability forecasting.

**For regulatory alignment**, build to ISO 42001 as the certifiable management system, map controls to EU AI Act requirements (particularly Articles 14 and 50 for August 2026 enforcement), align with NIST AI RMF's Govern-Map-Measure-Manage cycle, and engage with NIST's AI Agent Standards Initiative (comments due April 2026).

## Conclusion

The research landscape for agentic AI governance in early 2026 is remarkably rich but unevenly mature. Agent safety, multi-agent failure analysis, and trust calibration research have produced strong empirical foundations. Identity standards and zero trust frameworks have reached an actionable inflection point, with NIST, OpenID Foundation, and CSA providing complementary specifications. Observability and runtime verification tools — AgentSpec, AgentGuard, SentinelAgent, LlamaFirewall — are empirically validated but not yet widely deployed. **The most underdeveloped area is quantitative risk assessment for agents**: connecting autonomy levels, tool access scopes, and action irreversibility to probabilistic financial loss estimates remains an open problem that practitioners must address through adaptation rather than adoption.

The strategic imperative is to build now rather than wait. Gartner projects 40% enterprise agent penetration by end of 2026; NIST agent-specific standards will not finalize before 2027. The research provides sufficient building blocks — OWASP taxonomy, MAESTRO threat modeling, CSA ATF zero trust governance, NIST identity guidance, ISO 42001 management system, and Anthropic's empirical autonomy measurement — to construct a defensible, evidence-based governance framework today, with explicit extension points for standards that are still converging.
