# **The State of Security, Governance, and Risk Management Frameworks for Agentic AI Systems**

## **Introduction: The Agentic Paradigm Shift and the Governance Gap**

As of March 2026, the artificial intelligence landscape has undergone a profound architectural, operational, and structural transformation. The preceding years were characterized by the deployment of conversational, prompt-and-response large language models (LLMs) that functioned primarily as passive generators of text, code, and media. Today, the technological ecosystem is heavily dominated by "agentic AI" or autonomous AI agents. These are sophisticated, goal-driven systems designed to plan complex tasks, execute probabilistic reasoning, invoke external application programming interfaces (APIs), manipulate enterprise data stores, and independently execute multi-step workflows with minimal to no human intervention.1

This transition from generation to execution fundamentally alters the enterprise attack surface. In a purely generative paradigm, security and governance frameworks concentrated predominantly on preventing data exfiltration, mitigating algorithmic bias, and filtering malicious or toxic outputs.2 The primary concern was what the artificial intelligence might synthesize and display to a user. In the agentic paradigm, an AI system possesses distinct identity, delegated privileges, and the capacity to autonomously alter state within enterprise infrastructure.2 Consequently, an exploited or misaligned agent can unilaterally authorize financial transactions, execute remote code, poison retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) repositories, and trigger cascading systemic failures across deeply interdependent networks.1

The rapid commercial adoption of these systems has precipitated a severe "governance gap." Traditional cybersecurity architectures and early AI risk management frameworks—such as the initial iterations of the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) and the foundational drafts of the EU Artificial Intelligence Act—were predicated on the assumption that AI systems assisted human decision-making rather than independently executing decisions.7 Furthermore, empirical survey data from early 2026 reveals a critical disconnect between deployment and control. According to the Cloud Security Alliance (CSA), while 81% of enterprise teams have moved past the planning phase for agentic AI, a mere 14.4% of organizations have secured full security approval for their agent fleets, resulting in massive deployments of unmonitored "shadow AI".9

In response to this rapidly escalating risk environment, global standards bodies, regulatory agencies, and open-source foundations have spent the last year developing, revising, and harmonizing frameworks specifically tailored to the unique behavioral profiles of autonomous agents. This exhaustive research report analyzes the current state of agentic AI security, governance, and risk management standards as of March 2026\. It provides a comprehensive evaluation of the contributions from the Open Web Application Security Project (OWASP), the Cloud Security Alliance (CSA), the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), the International Organization for Standardization (ISO/IEC), the European Union, and emerging technical protocol consortiums operating under the Linux Foundation.

## **1\. The Agentic Threat Landscape: OWASP and MITRE ATLAS**

To effectively secure and govern autonomous systems, the cybersecurity community required a standardized taxonomy of the novel vulnerabilities introduced by agentic architecture. By late 2025 and early 2026, both OWASP and MITRE delivered dedicated, mature frameworks outlining how adversaries actively target autonomy, delegation chains, and runtime behavior in production environments.

## **1.1 The OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications (2026)**

In February 2026, the OWASP Agentic Security Initiative (ASI) formally published the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications.10 This release is widely adopted and represents a formal recognition that the prior OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications (v1.1)—which focused primarily on prompt injection (LLM01), insecure output handling (LLM02), and training data poisoning (LLM03)—was structurally insufficient for systems granted system-level privileges.11 The 2026 framework shifts the focus entirely from "what AI says" to "what AI does," providing a taxonomy of operational risks and actionable mitigation strategies.2

The critical risks identified in the published 2026 framework address the four interconnected domains of the agentic attack surface: the underlying model layer, the external tool ecosystem, the persistent memory architecture, and the inter-agent communication protocols.11

| Risk Designation | Vulnerability Title | Operational Impact and Description | Mitigation and Zero Trust Alignment |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **ASI01** | Agent Goal Hijack | Attackers manipulate an agent's fundamental objectives. Rather than injecting a single bad response, attackers use hidden instructions to permanently redirect the agent's goal structure while it appears compliant.1 | Lock and version system prompts; separate instruction context from external payloads; monitor for goal drift.1 Aligns with Zero Trust continuous verification. |
| **ASI02** | Tool Misuse & Exploitation | Agents leverage legitimate external tools (e.g., APIs, email clients) in unsafe ways due to ambiguous internal logic or malicious injection (e.g., sending sensitive documents externally).1 | Enforce least-privilege tool access; validate and authorize high-impact actions; maintain immutable auditability of all tool invocation chains.13 |
| **ASI03** | Identity & Privilege Abuse | Agents inherit broad human credentials or cache administrative tokens from prior workflows, allowing them to escalate privileges or move laterally across networks.13 | Transition to ephemeral, identity-based credentials; eliminate long-lived API keys; implement continuous runtime traceability.13 |
| **ASI04** | Agentic Supply Chain Vulnerabilities | Agents dynamically load plugins, prompts, or dependent tools at runtime. Tampering with a single external dependency compromises the autonomous workflow, leading to potential internal execution.13 | Maintain continuous inventories of AI components; validate component integrity; block untrusted sources; monitor lineage at runtime.13 |
| **ASI05** | Remote Code Execution (RCE) | Exploitation of the agent's environment to execute arbitrary, malicious code, often achieved through compromised toolchains or unvalidated external calls.14 | Isolate execution environments (sandboxing); tightly control shell access; monitor for unexpected external payloads.14 |
| **ASI06** | Memory & Context Injection | Attackers poison persistent memory stores, RAG vector databases, or session contexts to subtly steer long-term agent behavior, resulting in persistent misalignment.13 | Classify data prior to memory ingestion; block public access to core RAG repositories; detect attempts to manipulate retrieval mechanisms.13 |
| **ASI07** | Insecure Inter-Agent Communications | Exploitation of trust between multiple autonomous agents. Messages can be spoofed or tampered with, allowing a compromised low-privilege agent to manipulate a high-privilege orchestration agent.14 | Require cryptographic signatures for all inter-agent communications; enforce zero trust validation at the agent-to-agent boundary.14 |
| **ASI08** | Cascading Failures | A localized error or malicious manipulation in one agent triggers a multi-agent amplification loop, rapidly spreading operational failure across the enterprise via repeating requests.6 | Implement circuit breakers and threshold limits on agent actions; require human-in-the-loop approvals for anomalous high-frequency requests.6 |
| **ASI09** | Human-Agent Trust Exploitation | Agents generate legitimate-looking flows that pressure human users into harmful approvals, effectively executing social engineering via autonomous systems.14 | Implement strict verification protocols for high-stakes approvals; train users to recognize agentic manipulation patterns.14 |
| **ASI10** | Rogue Agents | Fully misaligned autonomous behavior where an agent operates entirely outside its intended parameters, behaving like a malicious insider with broad access attempts.14 | Deploy kill switches and containment protocols; establish rigorous behavioral baselines to detect unpredictable sequences rapidly.8 |

Beyond the Top 10, the OWASP Agentic Security Initiative (ASI) actively develops supplementary, mature projects to address multi-agent workflow governance. The "Multi-Agentic System Threat Modeling Guide v1.0" expands upon the core taxonomy to map out threats in complex topologies, such as LangGraph or CrewAI frameworks, where planner agents orchestrate executor agents in hierarchical structures.6 Furthermore, OWASP has contributed heavily to the HUMAN Verified AI Agent Project, demonstrating how the OWASP ANS identity framework and HTTP Message Signatures can be utilized to enforce Agent-to-Agent (A2A) trust, ensuring only cryptographically verified AI agents can execute transactions.19

**Key Gaps in OWASP:** While OWASP excels at identifying threats and providing tactical mitigation strategies, it functions primarily as an application security framework. It does not provide the comprehensive organizational management structures or legal compliance mapping required for enterprise-wide risk management, necessitating integration with broader frameworks like ISO 42001 or NIST.

## **1.2 MITRE ATLAS 2025/2026 Expansion**

Mirroring OWASP's shift toward execution-layer exposure, the Adversarial Threat Landscape for Artificial-Intelligence Systems (MITRE ATLAS) underwent a substantial evolution. In late 2025 and early 2026, MITRE researchers—in collaboration with industry partners like Zenity—added 14 new agent-focused techniques to the ATLAS framework, bringing the total to 66 techniques and 46 subtechniques specific to AI systems.21

The 2026 MITRE ATLAS updates explicitly address the reality that AI agents are now operational, privileged, and deeply embedded in enterprise workflows.21 The framework prioritizes the modeling of autonomous workflow chaining, delegated authority persistence, and API-level orchestration risks.21 A prominent and highly utilized addition is the "AI Agent Tool Credential Harvesting" technique, which formalizes how adversaries target agents connected to repositories like SharePoint or OneDrive.21 Because agents frequently store or access API keys and credentials to perform autonomous actions, compromising a single agent provides adversaries with illicit access to an extensive web of enterprise secrets.21

The consensus across both OWASP and MITRE in 2026 is unambiguous: the traditional cybersecurity kill chain has been drastically compressed. Threat modeling must no longer focus solely on the inputs and outputs of a monolithic model, but rather on how distributed AI entities interact with enterprise infrastructure over extended time horizons.2

## **2\. Zero Trust Architecture and Agent Identity**

The rapid deployment of autonomous systems has precipitated what the Cloud Security Alliance (CSA) explicitly termed an "identity crisis." According to the 2026 CSA "State of AI Agent Security" report, while deployment is massive, only 21.9% of enterprise teams treat AI agents as independent, identity-bearing entities.9 Alarmingly, 45.6% of teams continue to rely on shared API keys for agent-to-agent authentication, treating highly autonomous agents as generic, unmonitored service accounts.9

This reliance on static credentials creates unmonitored access pathways, allowing overprivileged agents to become the ultimate insider threat—capable of exfiltrating data at machine speed without ever compromising a human credential.15 To combat this, security frameworks have aggressively pivoted to apply Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) principles to non-human AI identities.

## **2.1 NIST SP 800-207 Applied to Agentic Workflows**

NIST Special Publication 800-207 provides the abstract definition and core tenets of Zero Trust Architecture, emphasizing the protection of resources rather than network segments.24 By 2026, these tenets have been explicitly adapted for multi-agent AI systems, demanding that trust is never implicitly granted based on network location or internal origination.25

The application of NIST SP 800-207 to AI agents mandates the following strict translations of its core tenets:

* **Tenet 2 (Secured Communication):** All AI system communications—including intra-cluster agent-to-agent (A2A) dialogue—must be secured, encrypted, and authenticated, neutralizing the legacy assumption that internal agent traffic is inherently safe.25  
* **Tenet 3 (Per-Session Access):** Access to enterprise resources by an agent is granted strictly on a per-session basis. Trust is evaluated dynamically for every specific action (e.g., retrieving a document or sending an email) rather than granting blanket permissions upon instantiation. Authorization to one resource does not automatically grant access to another.25  
* **Tenet 4 (Dynamic Policy):** Access is determined by dynamic policies that account equally for machine/agent identities and human users. Policies must incorporate behavioral signals, tool usage history, and environmental context.26  
* **Tenet 6 (Dynamic Authentication/Authorization):** Agents must verify their identity continuously. This is considered the fundamental antidote to the problem of AI agents accumulating excessive permissions over time. Implementations must include policy-based reauthentication.25  
* **Tenet 7 (Continuous Monitoring):** The enterprise collects extensive information about asset security posture and access requests, fine-tuning access policies according to the data collected.26

## **2.2 The NIST NCCoE Agent Identity Concept Paper**

To operationalize Zero Trust for agents, the NIST National Cybersecurity Center of Excellence (NCCoE) released a foundational concept paper in February 2026 titled "Accelerating the Adoption of Software and Artificial Intelligence Agent Identity and Authorization".28 The paper is currently in draft status, soliciting comments until April 2, 2026\.30 Recognizing that AI agents introduce unique challenges regarding delegation and non-repudiation, the NCCoE project explores how existing identity standards can be extended to autonomous software operating within enterprise boundaries.28

The NCCoE outlines specific technological pathways for establishing robust agent identity:

* **Cryptographic Workload Identity:** The paper explores utilizing frameworks like SPIFFE (Secure Production Identity Framework for Everyone) and SPIRE to issue ephemeral, cryptographic identities directly to agent workloads. This allows systems to verify an agent's authenticity cryptographically without relying on static, easily compromised API keys.28  
* **Delegation of Authority:** The paper advocates for leveraging OAuth 2.0/2.1 and OpenID Connect (OIDC) to manage how rights and entitlements are granted to software.28 A critical architectural challenge is linking a human user's identity to an agent to support "on behalf of" actions. The NCCoE explores binding agent identity with human identity to maintain accountability during "human-in-the-loop" authorizations.28  
* **Attribute-Based Access Control:** Employing standards like Next Generation Access Control (NGAC) to facilitate fine-grained, event-driven policy updates. This allows authorization parameters to shift instantaneously if an agent's context or behavior profile changes.28  
* **Lifecycle Management:** Utilizing the System for Cross-domain Identity Management (SCIM) for automating the provisioning and lifecycle management of agent identities across various systems.28

**Key Gaps in NCCoE Guidance:** The current draft explicitly excludes Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) architectures and architectures using only an LLM with its training data from its primary scope.28 Furthermore, it focuses strictly on enterprise use-cases, explicitly excluding the management of external, public-facing agents from untrusted sources in this initial phase.28

## **2.3 The CSA Agentic Trust Framework**

Complementing the NIST initiatives, the Cloud Security Alliance published the Agentic Trust Framework (ATF) in February 2026\.8 The ATF serves as an open governance specification aimed at addressing the "governance gap" caused by non-deterministic agent behavior. Traditional Zero Trust assumes deterministic system rules and human users with predictable behavior; the ATF adapts this for AI agents that make probabilistic decisions and alter their access needs dynamically.8

The ATF requires organizations to manage agents across five core domains to establish Agentic Zero Trust:

1. **Identity ("Who are you?"):** Enforcing immutable identifiers and cryptographically binding credentials to a declared purpose and capability manifest. This establishes a clear ownership chain.8  
2. **Behavior ("What are you doing?"):** Establishing behavioral baselines and utilizing structured logging to enable intent analysis, anomaly detection, and explainability for decisions.8  
3. **Data Governance ("What are you eating? What are you serving?"):** Implementing rigorous input validation, personally identifiable information (PII) protection, and output governance to protect against data leakage.8  
4. **Segmentation ("Where can you go?"):** Enforcing rigid resource boundaries and policy execution.8  
5. **Incident Response ("What if you go rogue?"):** Mandating the integration of automated circuit breakers, kill switches, and containment protocols in the event an agent diverges from its aligned goals.8

Crucially, the ATF introduces a maturity model based on "earned autonomy." Rather than treating access as a binary allowance, an agent's operational independence is tiered. New agents begin with highly restricted, heavily monitored permissions (akin to an "Intern" level) and systematically earn higher autonomy (e.g., "Principal" level) by successfully navigating promotion gates and demonstrating consistent, benign behavior over time.8

The OpenID Foundation has strongly supported this approach in their 2026 response to NIST, emphasizing that the most urgent AI agent security risks are failures of trust. They advocate for a foundational "trust fabric" that can automatically verify credentials and constrain actions across multiple organizations, moving away from makeshift, manually managed access lists.32

## **3\. Protocol Governance, Interoperability, and Observability**

Because enterprise value is derived from the orchestration of multiple specialized AI agents, standardizing how these agents connect to tools and to each other has become a critical governance priority. A fragmented ecosystem of custom API integrations prevents holistic security auditing and zero trust enforcement. By 2026, the industry consolidated its approach through open-source foundations and standardized semantic conventions.

## **3.1 The Linux Foundation and the Agentic AI Foundation**

In December 2025, the Linux Foundation announced the formation of the Agentic AI Foundation to serve as the unified governance home for the infrastructure layer of AI connectivity.33 This initiative represents a rare consensus among major competitors—including OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Anthropic, and AWS—to support common interoperability standards to prevent deep fragmentation.33

Two primary, published protocols govern this ecosystem:

* **Model Context Protocol (MCP):** Originally launched by Anthropic and donated to the Linux Foundation in December 2025, MCP standardizes how AI systems connect to external tools, data sources, and services.33 Utilizing JSON-RPC 2.0 transported over standard input/output (stdio) or Server-Sent Events (SSE), MCP functions as a universal plugin architecture with defined clients and servers.33  
* **Agent-to-Agent (A2A) Protocol:** Developed by Google and donated in June 2025, A2A facilitates secure, interoperable communication and collaboration directly between different AI agents, regardless of the underlying vendor or framework.33

To manage these protocols securely, the Linux Foundation welcomed the agentgateway project, which provides a centralized, secure management layer that natively understands both MCP and A2A.37 Additional initiatives include AGENTS.md, a universal standard providing AI coding agents with consistent project-specific guidance, and goose, an open-source, local-first AI agent framework.34

While MCP and A2A solve interoperability, they also centralize the attack surface. A 2026 whitepaper by the Coalition for Secure AI (CoSAI) highlighted that MCP introduces unique vulnerabilities because an LLM acts as an intermediary between user intent and system actions.38 CoSAI identified nearly 40 distinct threats across 12 categories targeting MCP architecture, emphasizing that traditional firewalls are easily bypassed when an AI agent can be manipulated through natural language prompt injections embedded in support tickets or external web pages.38 The governance of these protocols relies heavily on integrating them with the Zero Trust identity architectures established by NIST and CSA.

## **3.2 OpenTelemetry Generative AI Semantic Conventions**

The axiom "you cannot govern what you cannot see" is particularly challenging in the agentic era. Traditional application logging captures discrete, deterministic inputs and outputs. Agentic systems, however, execute probabilistic reasoning, looping over data, and spawning sub-tasks dynamically.39 To address this, the Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF) and the OpenTelemetry (OTel) project aggressively expanded their standards to cover Generative AI.

By early 2026, OpenTelemetry released version 1.40.0 of its GenAI semantic conventions, establishing a unified, standardized vocabulary for tracking prompts, token usage, tool calls, and model responses.40 These conventions are currently marked as "Development/Experimental," requiring instrumentations using v1.36.0 or prior to actively opt-in using the OTEL\_SEMCONV\_STABILITY\_OPT\_IN variable to access the latest features.40

A critical update in 2026 was the formalization of specific spans for agentic operations. OpenTelemetry defines an agent as a combination of reasoning, logic, and access to external information connected to a GenAI model.40

| Span/Operation Name | OpenTelemetry Definition and Implementation Details |
| :---- | :---- |
| create\_agent | Describes the creation or instantiation of a GenAI agent, particularly when interfacing with remote agent services (e.g., AWS Bedrock Agents). Requires gen\_ai.operation.name and gen\_ai.provider.name. Conditionally requires gen\_ai.agent.id and gen\_ai.agent.version. These spans are typically tagged with a CLIENT kind.40 |
| invoke\_agent | Describes the active invocation and execution cycle of an agent. Requires gen\_ai.operation.name. Crucially, it captures gen\_ai.conversation.id to maintain correlation across highly complex, multi-threaded reasoning loops. This ensures that every action taken by an agent can be traced back to a specific session and user intent.40 |
| execute\_tool | Captures the specific instance when an agent invokes an external function, API, or MCP server. This delineates the boundary between the AI's cognitive reasoning loop and its physical interaction with enterprise infrastructure, forming the basis for tool misuse auditing and Zero Trust enforcement.35 |

OpenTelemetry explicitly addresses the deployment topologies of agents through "Span Kinds." A CLIENT span kind is recommended when the agent runs in a separate process or communicates over HTTP, whereas an INTERNAL span kind is utilized for in-process orchestration frameworks like LangChain or CrewAI.40 Furthermore, the conventions contain dedicated guidelines for tracing Model Context Protocol (MCP) communications, recommending the use of domain-specific MCP conventions over standard RPC conventions to capture complex message exchanges within streaming calls.35 The widespread adoption of these OTel conventions transforms autonomous agents from opaque liabilities into observable, measurable assets, satisfying the strict audit trail requirements demanded by emerging regulations.41

**Key Gaps in Observability:** While OpenTelemetry provides the schema, the complexity of correlating multi-agent, asynchronous workflows across disparate enterprise boundaries remains technically challenging, requiring vendors to build sophisticated analysis engines on top of the raw OTel data.45

## **4\. National and Sector-Specific Risk Management**

As the technical underpinnings of agent identity, connectivity, and observability coalesced, national bodies modernized their broader risk management postures to accommodate dual-use and highly autonomous capabilities.

## **4.1 The Evolution of the NIST AI Risk Management Framework**

The original NIST Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0 / NIST AI 100-1), published in early 2023, established the foundational GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE functions.46 However, as agentic capabilities expanded rapidly, NIST recognized the need for highly specialized profiles.

In July 2024, NIST released the Generative AI Profile (NIST AI 600-1) to address the unique risks of foundation models.46 This profile outlined specific actions, such as MS-1.1-005 (evaluating methods for measuring risks in offensive cyber and CBRN) and MG-1.3-001 (documenting risk trade-offs and staged release approaches).46 While highly relevant, the Generative AI Profile primarily targeted the synthesis of content and algorithmic monocultures.46

To directly confront the risks of systems taking action, NIST published the second public draft of NIST AI 800-1 in January 2025, titled "Managing Misuse Risk for Dual-Use Foundation Models".46 This guidance is particularly applicable to agentic systems that could lower the barrier to entry for offensive cyber operations, evade human control, or interact autonomously with physical environments and IoT mechanical systems.46 It outlines shared supply chain responsibilities, placing specific requirements on initial developers, compute providers, downstream model adapters, and distribution platforms.46

Furthermore, the NIST AI 100-2e2025 report, "Adversarial Machine Learning: A Taxonomy and Terminology of Attacks and Mitigations," provides the definitive, mature lexicon for attacks against AI.46 The 2025 update categorizes attacks based on the adversary's knowledge (White-box, Gray-box, Black-box) and details evasion, privacy breaches (e.g., Membership Inference, Likelihood Ratio Attacks/LiRA), and poisoning techniques (Availability and Backdoor/Trojaning).46 For agentic systems, data poisoning is a critical threat vector; an attacker can execute a clean-label backdoor attack by injecting semantic triggers into a vector database, permanently altering an agent's reasoning logic without triggering standard anomaly detection tools.6

## **4.2 The CAISI AI Agent Standards Initiative**

Recognizing that generic AI guidance was insufficient for the autonomy era, the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), housed within NIST, formally launched the AI Agent Standards Initiative in February 2026\.50 The initiative is driven by the understanding that absent public confidence in the reliability and secure interoperability of agents, their real-world utility and adoption will be severely stunted.50

The foundation of this initiative is the January 2026 Request for Information (RFI) regarding security considerations for AI agents.46 The RFI explicitly requested ecosystem insights across five focus pillars 46:

| CAISI RFI Pillar | Focus Area for Agentic Security |
| :---- | :---- |
| **Pillar 1: Threats, Risks, Vulnerabilities** | Identifying unique threats distinct from traditional software, varying by model capability, tool use, and multi-agent system configurations.46 |
| **Pillar 2: Security Practices** | Actionable recommendations for design, training, and deployment, emphasizing technical mitigations like system isolation, tool access controls, and human-in-the-loop oversight.46 |
| **Pillar 3: Assessing Security** | Methodologies for benchmarking and red-teaming, specifically distinguishing between open-source and closed-source AI model assessment.46 |
| **Pillar 4: Deployment Environments** | Investigating technical means to tightly constrain access to the environment where the agent acts, including implementing undo, rollback, or negation mechanisms for unwanted autonomous actions.46 |
| **Pillar 5: Systemic Challenges** | Identifying gaps in technical standards and setting research priorities to advance the scientific understanding of agent-specific vulnerabilities.46 |

CAISI's internal technical research has highlighted the extreme urgency of these protections. In late 2025, CAISI researchers improved the open-source AgentDojo evaluation framework (developed by ETH Zurich) to test agents against remote code execution, automated phishing, and database exfiltration tasks.46 Their red-teaming exercises demonstrated that when attackers employ multi-attempt, optimized attacks against agentic LLMs (such as Claude 3.5 Sonnet), the attack success rate for "agent hijacking" (indirect prompt injection) surged from a baseline of 11% to over 81%.46 This empirical evidence directly fueled the aggressive push for the Zero Trust authorization standards outlined in the NCCoE concept paper.

## **4.3 CSA MAESTRO and Threat Modeling**

In tandem with NIST's efforts, the Cloud Security Alliance continued to evolve its AI Safety Initiative. Recognizing that legacy benchmarks fail to capture the cascading risks of autonomous agents, CSA developed the MAESTRO Agentic AI threat modeling framework.53 By early 2026, enterprise security teams began adopting benchmarks based on MAESTRO to test the limits of agentic workflow execution.53 Version 2 (MAESTRO v2) is slated for release later in 2026 to provide detailed, environment-specific implementation guidance and practical instructions for vendors.53 MAESTRO serves to operationalize threat modeling at the execution layer, assessing the risk of chained vulnerabilities that emerge only when an agent iterates through multiple tool calls autonomously.21

## **5\. International Standards and Management Systems (ISO/IEC & IEEE)**

By 2026, the global AI governance narrative has decisively shifted from voluntary, aspirational principles to mandatory, auditable execution.39 Static policy statements have been replaced by the rigorous demands of hard law and certifiable international standards.

## **5.1 ISO/IEC 42001 and Agentic Adaptation**

Published in late 2023, ISO/IEC 42001 (Artificial Intelligence — Management System) emerged as the premier, widely adopted certifiable framework for AI governance globally.55 It integrates seamlessly with established paradigms like ISO/IEC 27001 (Information Security Management) and ISO/IEC 27701 (Privacy Information Management), converting high-level responsible AI principles into structured, audit-ready controls covering risk assessments, human oversight, bias mitigation, and continuous monitoring.55 A system processing personal data for automated decisions requires all three: ISO 27001 securing the infrastructure, privacy frameworks governing the data, and ISO 42001 managing AI-specific risks like unexplainable decisions or model drift.59

However, by 2026, organizations discovered that applying ISO/IEC 42001 to agentic systems requires paradigm shifts in how evidence is collected and risk is defined.39 Traditional AI governance assumed centralized models performing batch predictions. Agentic AI involves dynamic systems embedded deeply into core business applications, often via vendor-integrated copilots that autonomously access enterprise data.39 Consequently, compliance teams are heavily utilizing complementary standards, such as ISO/IEC 23894 (AI Risk Management), to map the specific algorithmic and socio-technical risks of autonomous decision-making across the lifecycle.55 Furthermore, ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 42 (the joint technical committee on AI) continues to expand its massive program of work, generating technical specifications on AI system life cycle processes (ISO/IEC 5338), use cases (ISO/IEC TR 24030), and human-machine teaming (ISO/IEC 25589).61

## **5.2 IEEE Governance Standards**

The Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) has formalized its approach to AI governance through the P7000 series and several highly focused projects under the Artificial Intelligence Standards Committee (C/AISC).65

In 2026, several IEEE standards are critical for providing technical depth to agentic oversight:

* **IEEE P2863:** Recommended Practice for Organizational Governance of Artificial Intelligence. Currently in the "SA Ballot: Comment Resolution" phase, this framework establishes the overall management structure required to integrate ethics, safety, and accountability into AI operations. It uniquely provides case studies for resolving tensions between differing governance principles.65  
* **IEEE P3394:** Standard for Large Language Model Agent Interface (in Draft Development). Focuses specifically on the interface layer for agentic systems.65  
* **IEEE P3709:** Standard for Framework and Technical Requirements of Agentic Artificial Intelligence (in Draft Development). Directly addresses the technical architecture of autonomous agents.65  
* **IEEE P7999:** Standard for Integrating Organizational Ethics Oversight in Projects and Processes Involving Artificial Intelligence (in Draft Development).65

These standards are essential for organizations seeking to prove that their deployment of autonomous agents meets global engineering consensus for safety and reliability, acting as a technical bridge between high-level management systems and actual code execution.

## **5.3 The Emergence of AIUC-1**

Because ISO/IEC 42001 focuses broadly on the management system itself (the policies, committees, and documentation), a distinct and urgent need arose for a framework capable of certifying the actual technical execution and behavioral reliability of AI agents acting within enterprise systems.4 This led to the rapid adoption of AIUC-1 in 2026\.5

AIUC-1 is not a general model development standard; it explicitly treats AI agents as non-human actors operating inside the corporate control environment.5 It provides a single, auditable framework for delegated authority, validating that AI agents behave safely under pressure, in adversarial conditions, and when handling sensitive data.4 By combining independent audits with intensive technical testing (e.g., thousands of adversarial robustness and input-filtering evaluations), AIUC-1 certifies the operational safety of agentic automation. Major industry players, such as UiPath and Cisco, have aligned with AIUC-1 to operationalize their AI security frameworks and demonstrate compliance to enterprise clients.4

## **6\. Regulatory Mandates and The EU AI Act**

In Europe, the Artificial Intelligence Act has transitioned completely from legislative negotiation to strict, operational enforcement. The legal landscape in 2026 demands precise compliance, transforming agentic governance into a matter of profound legal and financial consequence.54

## **6.1 Enforcement Timelines and High-Risk Requirements**

The application timeline of the AI Act defines the regulatory environment. Prohibitions on unacceptable risk AI practices and AI literacy obligations entered into force in February 2025\.39 The governance rules and obligations for General-Purpose AI (GPAI) models—which include foundational models underlying most agentic systems—became fully applicable in August 2025, governed by the detailed GPAI Code of Practice.74

However, the most significant deadline for enterprise systems arrives on **August 2, 2026**.39 On this date, the vast majority of provisions become applicable, and all systems classified as High-Risk AI systems (under Annex III and Articles 9–15) must comply with core requirements.75 An extended deadline of August 2027 applies only to high-risk AI systems embedded as safety components in products already regulated under EU product safety laws (e.g., medical devices, machinery).74

For High-Risk systems, providers must establish continuous risk management systems, ensure high-quality data governance to minimize discriminatory outcomes, maintain rigorous logging for traceability, provide detailed technical documentation, and ensure a high level of robustness and cybersecurity.74

Crucially, the Act mandates appropriate **human oversight measures** (Article 14).74 Agentic systems pose profound compliance challenges under this requirement. The law demands "effective human oversight," yet the fundamental business logic and economic value of agentic AI is to eliminate human friction and operate autonomously.79 Organizations must architect systems where human-in-the-loop interventions can be securely inserted into probabilistic workflows without destroying the agent's utility or creating massive operational bottlenecks.

## **6.2 CEN/CENELEC and Harmonized Standards**

To provide legal certainty and a clear pathway to compliance, the European Commission tasked the European Committee for Standardization (CEN) and the European Committee for Electrotechnical Standardization (CENELEC) via Joint Technical Committee 21 (JTC 21\) with drafting harmonized European standards.80 Conforming to a harmonized standard published in the Official Journal of the European Union grants providers a "presumption of conformity" with the applicable parts of the AI Act.81

The Commission requested standards across ten key areas, including risk management, data governance, record keeping, transparency, human oversight, robustness, and cybersecurity.80 However, translating the broad legal requirements of the AI Act into highly specific technical specifications for rapidly evolving agentic systems proved immensely difficult. A significant point of friction involves adapting international ISO/IEC standards to meet the strict fundamental rights and constitutional values mandated by EU law, often requiring the creation of European-specific 'Z-annexes' or entirely new European Standards (EN) when international consensus falls short.83

Recognizing the looming August 2026 enforcement deadline and the complexity of the task, CEN and CENELEC adopted an exceptional package of measures during their Technical Board meetings in October 2025 to drastically accelerate delivery.84 To ensure the foundational standards are available by Q4 2026, the Boards agreed to bypass separate Formal Votes, allowing direct publication of drafts following a positive Enquiry vote.84 Furthermore, they established small, dedicated drafting groups to aggressively finalize the most delayed drafts.84 This compressed timeline and deployment of emergency procedures highlights the intense regulatory pressure governing the deployment of autonomous systems in 2026\.73

## **7\. Synthesis: The Landscape, Gaps, and the Path Forward**

As of March 2026, the landscape of agentic governance and security has matured from a period of theoretical panic into structured, albeit highly complex, engineering disciplines. A clear consensus has formed around several indispensable principles, yet significant gaps remain that demand further innovation to secure the autonomous enterprise.

## **7.1 Where Consensus is Forming**

1. **Identity is the Perimeter:** The cybersecurity community unequivocally agrees that traditional network boundaries and shared API keys are obsolete and dangerous for agentic systems.9 Cryptographic, workload-specific identities (such as those facilitated by SPIFFE/SPIRE) governed by strict Zero Trust tenets (NIST SP 800-207) form the required baseline for all agentic architectures.8  
2. **Continuous, Dynamic Validation:** Because agentic outputs are probabilistic and highly dependent on shifting contexts, trust cannot be granted statically at the beginning of a session. Security requires continuous intent validation, attribute-based access control, token diminishment through delegation chains, and behavioral anomaly detection, as outlined in the CSA Agentic Trust Framework and NIST NCCoE guidance.8  
3. **Standardized Observability:** The integration of OpenTelemetry GenAI semantic conventions represents a major victory for operational governance. Standardizing the telemetry of create\_agent, invoke\_agent, and execute\_tool spans ensures that organizations can maintain the robust, granular audit trails demanded by the EU AI Act and ISO/IEC 42001\.39

## **7.2 The Agentic Governance Gap**

Despite these advancements, the ecosystem continues to grapple with what experts define as the "Agentic Governance Gap".7 No single existing standard or framework comprehensively solves the following challenges:

* **Probabilistic Auditing and Accountability:** While frameworks demand logging and auditability, interpreting the *intent* behind an autonomous, multi-step LLM decision chain remains profoundly difficult. When an agent hallucinates a goal sub-task, invokes a tool, and executes a detrimental transaction, tracing legal accountability across the tool vendor, the orchestration developer, and the foundation model provider requires novel legal and technical synthesis that ISO 42001 does not fully resolve.39  
* **Inter-Agent Contracts and Trust at Scale:** The emergence of the Agent-to-Agent (A2A) and Model Context Protocol (MCP) points to a future of vast, decentralized agent economies.33 However, verifying trust when an internal enterprise agent negotiates autonomously with an external, third-party agent involves complex cryptographic and semantic validation. While the OpenID Foundation emphasizes the need for a ubiquitous "trust fabric," the practical implementation of zero-trust boundaries between highly heterogeneous, multi-vendor agent systems remains in its infancy.16  
* **The Paradox of Oversight:** Regulatory frameworks like the EU AI Act strictly require human oversight, yet economic incentives drive full autonomy.74 Current frameworks lack standardized technical design patterns for implementing effective "circuit breakers" or "undo mechanisms" that interrupt machine-speed execution gracefully without destroying operational efficiency.8

## **7.3 Requirements for a Comprehensive Framework**

A truly comprehensive framework for the agentic era—one that bridges the gaps between OWASP's tactical threat taxonomy, NIST's identity controls, and the EU's strict regulatory mandates—must achieve holistic integration across the entire technology stack.

It must synthesize the rigorous threat-modeling of execution layers and delegation chains (MITRE ATLAS and CSA MAESTRO) 21 with the infrastructural protocols governing tool invocation and inter-agent communication (MCP and A2A).33 Furthermore, it must transition AI governance from a static compliance exercise reliant on policy documentation (ISO/IEC 42001\) 56 into real-time, deterministic control planes that monitor and constrain probabilistic agents continuously (AIUC-1).5

Ultimately, securing the agentic frontier in 2026 relies not on restraining the capability of artificial intelligence, but on meticulously weaving a cryptographic, observable, and dynamically authorized trust fabric around every single action an autonomous system takes.
